BUILDINGS & HOMES

Parmelee Farmhouse

The Town of Killingworth purchased the 131-acre property now known as Parmelee Farm in 2000. Today, the land serves as a community gathering place, with gardens, walking trails, and space for public events.

The farmhouse and garage are leased to the Killingworth Historical Society, which is responsible for their maintenance and preservation.

Throughout the mid-nineteenth century, the Parmelees operated one of the more productive farms in Killingworth. Agricultural census records from 1850 show the property encompassed approximately 110 acres and was valued among the higher farms in town.

The farmhouse was built in 1847 and became the home of Horace and Eunice Parmelee. Horace L. Parmelee, born in 1819, was the son of Moses and Ruth Parmelee. Eunice Maria Parmelee, born in 1822, was the daughter of Rufus and Eunice Parmelee.

Married in 1843, Horace and Eunice settled into the newly built house and raised their family while farming the surrounding land.

Throughout the mid-nineteenth century, the Parmelees operated one of the more productive farms in Killingworth. Agricultural census records from 1850 show the property encompassed approximately 110 acres and was valued among the higher farms in town.

The farm produced a wide range of crops and livestock, supporting both household needs and additional income. Farming at Parmelee Farm reflected the scale and diversity typical of prosperous rural life in nineteenth-century Connecticut.

Mrs. Parmelee in front of the house, 1899

In the early twentieth century, the farm passed through several owners and continued agricultural use, including dairy and poultry operations. Over time, changing economic conditions led to the gradual decline of farming on the property.

With the Town’s purchase of the land in 2000, Parmelee Farm entered a new chapter—preserved not as a working farm, but as a place where Killingworth’s agricultural and community history can be remembered and shared.
